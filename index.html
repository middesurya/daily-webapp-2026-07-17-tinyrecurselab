TinyRecurseLab — train a tiny recursive reasoner that thinks in loops

A ~17k-weight network can learn to solve Sudoku not by being big, but by recursing on a latent scratchpad — improving a draft answer over and over. That's the "Less is More" idea behind Tiny Recursive Models, which beat frontier LLMs on hard puzzles with 0.01% of the parameters. Everything here is real computation: a from-scratch autograd engine trains an actual model, live, on your machine.

Grounded in: Jolicoeur-Martineau, "Less is More: Recursive Reasoning with Tiny Networks", arXiv:2510.04871 (2025) · Wang et al., "Hierarchical Reasoning Model", arXiv:2506.21734 (2025)

The recursion, step by step latent refinement

The model keeps two things: y, its current guess at the full solution, and z, a latent "scratchpad" of private working memory. Each improvement step re-reads the puzzle x, updates the scratchpad several times, then nudges the answer. Watch a real trained model tighten a draft into a solved grid — and watch its scratchpad churn while it "thinks."

Deep supervision means the loss is applied after every improvement step, teaching the model to make progress each loop. Test-time compute: think longer, solve more recursion = compute

Once trained, you can run more improvement steps at inference than during training. This is the recursive analogue of an LLM "thinking longer." We sweep the number of steps and measure accuracy on held-out puzzles — revealing both the gains from extra recursion and the point where a tiny model starts to over-think and drift.

TRM vs HRM — why less is more runnable ablation

HRM used two networks recursing at different frequencies and a one-step gradient approximation. TRM's finding: a single tiny 2-layer net, back-propagated through the whole recursion, generalizes better and is far simpler. Adaptive halting — spend compute where it's needed ACT

Not every puzzle needs the same amount of thinking. Both HRM and TRM learn an adaptive computation time mechanism: keep looping until the answer stops changing (or a confidence/halt signal fires), then stop. Here we halt each puzzle when its predicted grid stabilizes, and plot how many steps different difficulties actually consume. Harder puzzles (more blanks) demand more loops.

This is the honest, gradient-free version of ACT: halt on solution stability. HRM instead trains a Q-head to predict "halt vs continue"; TRM uses a learned halt probability. Same goal — don't burn compute once you're confident.
